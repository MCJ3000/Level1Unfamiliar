NCEA Level 1 English

Achievement Standard 1.4: Unfamiliar Texts Revision

Welcome to Your Revision Guide!

This guide is designed to help you prepare for NCEA Level 1 English Achievement Standard 1.4: "Analyse significant aspects of unfamiliar texts through close reading, supported by evidence."

The key to success in this standard is to develop strong analytical skills and to be able to identify and explain how different elements of a text contribute to its meaning and purpose, even when you've never seen the text before.

Key Concepts & What to Look For

1. Purpose

What is the text trying to achieve? Why was it written?

  • To inform (e.g., news article, factual report)
  • To persuade (e.g., advertisement, opinion piece, speech)
  • To entertain (e.g., story, poem, song lyrics)
  • To instruct (e.g., recipe, manual)
  • To describe (e.g., descriptive passage, travelogue)

2. Audience

Who is the text written for? How do you know?

  • Age group (children, teenagers, adults)
  • Interests (sports fans, scientists, general public)
  • Level of knowledge (expert, beginner)
  • Relationship (friends, colleagues, customers)
  • Consider vocabulary, complexity, references.

3. Language Features

How does the writer use language to achieve their purpose and affect the audience?

  • Figurative Language: Metaphor, Simile, Personification, Hyperbole, Alliteration, Onomatopoeia, Imagery.
  • Word Choice (Diction): Connotations, Denotations, formal/informal, emotive language, jargon.
  • Sentence Structure: Short/long sentences, simple/complex sentences, rhetorical questions, imperative verbs.
  • Sound Devices: Rhyme, Rhythm, Assonance, Consonance (especially in poetry).
  • Tone: The writer's attitude towards the subject (e.g., serious, humorous, sarcastic, critical).

4. Structure & Form

How is the text organised? What is its overall shape?

  • Poetry: Stanzas, line breaks, rhyme scheme, rhythm.
  • Prose: Paragraphs, chapters, narrative arc (beginning, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution).
  • Non-fiction: Headings, subheadings, bullet points, introduction, body paragraphs, conclusion.
  • Genre conventions: Does it follow typical features of a news report, a short story, a speech?

Strategy: How to Approach an Unfamiliar Text

  1. 1. First Read-Through (Get the Gist)

    Read the text quickly to get a general understanding. Don't worry about details yet. What's it broadly about? What's your initial impression?

  2. 2. Second Read-Through (Annotate & Identify)

    Read more carefully. Highlight or make notes on:

    • Any words you don't understand (try to infer meaning from context).
    • Key ideas or arguments.
    • Examples of language features (metaphors, similes, strong verbs, emotive words).
    • Structural elements (e.g., how paragraphs flow, where a new idea is introduced).
    • Anything that stands out or seems significant.

  3. 3. Analyse (Connect & Explain)

    For each significant aspect you identified, ask yourself:

    • What is the feature/technique? (e.g., "The writer uses a metaphor...")
    • Where is it in the text? (Quote directly from the text as evidence.)
    • How does it work? (Explain its effect on the reader or its contribution to the meaning/purpose.)
    • Why is it effective? (Link it back to the writer's overall purpose or message.)

    Example Sentence Structure for Analysis:

    "The writer uses [language feature/structural element], such as '[direct quote from text]', to [explain how it works/its effect]. This effectively [link to purpose/audience/meaning]."

  4. 4. Synthesise (Overall Understanding)

    Bring your analysis together. How do all these elements work in combination to create the text's overall meaning, message, or effect?

Common Unfamiliar Text Types

Poetry

Focus on imagery, figurative language, sound devices (rhyme, rhythm, alliteration), tone, structure (stanzas, line breaks), and the emotional impact.

Prose Fiction (Short Story Excerpt)

Look at characterisation, setting, plot development, narrative voice, dialogue, descriptive language, and themes.

Non-Fiction (News Article, Opinion Piece, Speech)

Identify main arguments, evidence used, rhetorical devices, bias, tone, target audience, and overall message. Consider the use of facts, statistics, anecdotes.

Informative Texts (Fact Sheet, Report)

Examine clarity, organisation (headings, bullet points), use of technical language, objectivity, and how information is presented for easy understanding.

Song Lyrics

Consider poetic devices, narrative, emotional content, rhythm, rhyme, and how the lyrics convey a message or story.

Short Examples: Text, Questions & Responses

Example 1: Poetry

Text Excerpt:

"The old house sighed in the wind's cold embrace,

Windows like vacant eyes, staring into space.

A whisper of memories, a forgotten tune,

Beneath the pale glow of a silver moon."

Example Questions:

  • Identify and explain one language feature used in the excerpt.
  • What is the overall tone created by the poet in these lines? Support your answer with evidence.

Example Responses:

Response 1 (Language Feature):

The poet uses personification in the line "The old house sighed in the wind's cold embrace." This gives the house human qualities, suggesting it is tired or sad, which helps to create a melancholic atmosphere and makes the house seem alive and suffering from the weather.

Response 2 (Tone):

The overall tone created by the poet is melancholic and slightly eerie. This is evident through phrases like "vacant eyes," "forgotten tune," and the personification of the house "sighing." These elements suggest a sense of emptiness and past sadness, contributing to a somber mood.

Example 2: Prose Fiction

Text Excerpt:

"The bustling market square was a kaleidoscope of colours and sounds. Vendors shouted their wares, a cacophony of voices rising above the clatter of carts and the distant melody of a street musician. A scent of exotic spices mingled with fresh bread, drawing passersby deeper into the vibrant chaos."

Example Questions:

  • How does the writer use imagery to create a vivid sense of the market square?
  • What is the effect of the word "cacophony" on the reader's understanding of the market?

Example Responses:

Response 1 (Imagery):

The writer uses strong sensory imagery to bring the market to life. Visual imagery like "kaleidoscope of colours" and auditory imagery such as "cacophony of voices" and "clatter of carts" immerse the reader in the scene. The olfactory imagery of "scent of exotic spices mingled with fresh bread" further enhances the reader's experience, making the market feel vibrant and overwhelming.

Response 2 (Word Choice):

The word "cacophony" effectively conveys the overwhelming and somewhat disorderly nature of the market's sounds. It suggests a harsh and discordant mix of noises, rather than a pleasant blend. This choice of word makes the reader understand that the market is loud and perhaps a little chaotic, contributing to the sense of "vibrant chaos" mentioned later in the text.

Top Tips for Success

  • Practice Regularly: The more unfamiliar texts you read and analyse, the better you'll become.
  • Read Widely: Expose yourself to different genres, styles, and purposes of writing.
  • Build Vocabulary: A strong vocabulary helps you understand texts more easily.
  • Time Management: In an exam, allocate your time wisely across the texts and questions.
  • Use Evidence: Always support your points with direct quotes from the text.
  • Explain "How" and "Why": Don't just identify a feature; explain *how* it works and *why* the writer used it.
  • Stay Calm: It's called "unfamiliar" for a reason! Trust your skills and apply the strategies.
